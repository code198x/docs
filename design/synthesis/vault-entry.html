Rob Hubbard

Composer & programmer · b. 1955, Kingston upon Hull · the SID years
📷 portrait
(at the C64, mid-80s)
"Three voices, and no business sounding like a band."

In 1985 the Commodore 64 had three voices and no business sounding like a full band. Rob Hubbard made it sound like one anyway — and in doing so turned the game musician from an afterthought buried in the credits into a name printed on the box.

Hubbard's trick was to treat the SID 6581 as an instrument to be played, not a chip to be programmed. Where others wrote a tune and let it loop, he wrote a tiny program that performed one: arpeggios fast enough to fake a chord from a single voice, a drum stolen from a channel for a sixtieth of a second and handed back before your ear caught it, playback routines running several times a frame to pack in detail nobody believed the hardware had.

He didn't compose for the SID. He composed the SID.

The results — Monty on the Run, Commando, Sanxion, Thing on a Spring — were the reason a generation hummed game music in the playground. By the decade's end he'd moved to California and Electronic Arts, helping invent what an "audio director" even was. The bedroom had become an industry, and he'd helped build the door.
