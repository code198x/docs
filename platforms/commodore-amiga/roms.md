# Commodore Amiga — Getting the ROMs

**Purpose:** Explain to learners why the Amiga needs system ROMs, and how to obtain them legally
**Audience:** Amiga curriculum learners (and curriculum authors writing setup copy)
**Last Updated:** 2026-06-02

---

## A note on ROMs

To run anything on an Amiga — real or emulated — you need the machine's **system ROMs**:
**Kickstart** (its boot ROM) and **Workbench** (its desktop), the built-in software that makes the
machine a machine. An emulator without them is an empty shell.

Here's the honest part. **This course is free, and always will be — but the ROMs aren't ours to give
away.** They're still owned (today, by a company called Cloanto, who've kept these platforms legally
alive for decades), and we're not going to hand out copies of someone else's work to save you a
step. Preserving these machines *properly* — with respect for the people and the history behind them
— is the whole point of this project. Quietly pirating the ROMs would rather undercut that.

So you'll bring your own. It's cheaper and easier than it sounds, and you only do it once.

## Your options

1. **Want it to just work? — Amiga Forever.** Cloanto's
   [Amiga Forever](https://www.amigaforever.com/) (Plus edition, ~€40) gives you every Kickstart
   version plus Workbench, legally and reliably. For most learners without real hardware this is the
   path — and it's the exact setup this course is built and tested against.
2. **Own a real Amiga?** Dump your Kickstart ROM from it — it's yours.
3. **The free option, honestly — AROS.** There's a free, open Kickstart replacement
   ([AROS](https://aros.sourceforge.io/)). It's genuinely clever, but it can't boot Workbench 3.1
   and many programs won't run on it, so it won't carry you through this track. We mention it for
   honesty, not as the route we'd point you to.

## In the in-browser playground

For the Amiga you'll drop in your own ROM the first time — it stays in your browser and never
touches our servers.
