# Blitz Game 03: Craterfall

**Track:** Blitz Basic 2
**Genre:** Destructible terrain artillery game
**Units:** 64 (4 phases × 16 units)
**Language:** Blitz Basic 2
**Max lines:** ~500

---

## Premise

Two players (or player vs AI) take turns firing projectiles across a landscape. Projectiles arc through the air and explode on impact, deforming the terrain — blasting craters into the hillside. Adjust angle and power, account for wind, and try to hit the other player's position. The terrain is a bitmap that gets destroyed in real-time.

This is the Worms/Scorched Earth lineage. Worms was written in Blitz Basic — proof that this genre belongs here. The game teaches bitmap manipulation, physics simulation, and the performance that makes destructible terrain possible.

---

## Core Mechanics

### The Terrain

A bitmap landscape: rolling hills generated by a midpoint displacement algorithm or sine-wave combination. The terrain is drawn as filled regions — green hills, brown earth beneath, blue sky above. Everything is pixels on a bitmap.

Explosions modify the bitmap directly: a circle of pixels is cleared to sky colour. The terrain is permanently deformed. Over many turns, the landscape becomes cratered and broken. Players can fall into craters or be buried by collapsing terrain.

### Projectile Physics

The player sets angle (0-180°) and power (0-100%). The projectile follows a parabolic arc:

```
x = x + vx
y = y + vy
vy = vy + gravity
vx = vx + wind
```

The projectile is drawn frame-by-frame as it arcs through the air (Blitz renders this smoothly). On impact (projectile pixel hits non-sky terrain), explosion.

### Explosions

A circle of radius R (depends on weapon) is cleared from the terrain bitmap. Any player within the blast radius takes damage proportional to distance from centre. The crater is permanent.

Particle effects: debris pixels scatter outward from the explosion centre. Compiled Blitz makes 50+ particles smooth.

### Wind

A random wind value per turn, displayed as an arrow. Affects horizontal projectile velocity. Strong wind requires compensation. Adds unpredictability.

### Players

2-4 players (local hot-seat) or player vs AI. Each player has:
- Position on the terrain
- Health (0-100)
- Current weapon
- Angle and power settings from previous turn (remembered)

If the terrain beneath a player is destroyed, they fall until they land on solid ground. If they fall off the screen bottom, they're eliminated.

### Weapons

| Weapon | Blast radius | Damage | Special |
|--------|-------------|--------|---------|
| Standard shell | Medium | Medium | Default, unlimited |
| Big bomb | Large | High | 3 per game |
| Cluster bomb | Small × 5 | Low each | Splits into 5 bomblets at apex |
| Dirt bomb | Medium | None | ADDS terrain instead of removing it (bury the opponent) |
| Napalm | Wide, shallow | Medium over time | Burns across the surface |

### Phase 3-4: Polish and Production (Units 33-64)

Additional terrain types (desert, arctic, volcanic — different colour palettes). Tournament mode (best of 5 rounds, terrain regenerates each round). Statistics (damage dealt per player, most destructive weapon, biggest combo). Replay system (store shots, replay the decisive moments). Title screen with terrain generation demo. High score / win tracker. Bootable ADF. System-friendly startup/shutdown.

Key technical highlights:
- Bitmap manipulation at scale (clearing/adding hundreds of pixels per explosion)
- 50+ simultaneous particles rendered smoothly (compiled advantage)
- AI trajectory calculation using projectile physics in reverse
- Per-pixel terrain collision (ReadPixel for impact detection)
- The Worms connection: "This genre was commercially viable in Blitz Basic"

## Design Notes

### Why Destructible Terrain

Bitmap manipulation is the technique that separates Blitz from AMOS in terms of raw capability. AMOS can do sprite games well, but modifying hundreds of pixels per frame — clearing craters, scattering particles, running physics on 50 objects — pushes interpreted BASIC past its limits. Compiled Blitz handles it without breaking a sweat.

The genre also connects to Amiga history: Worms, the most commercially successful Blitz Basic game, is a destructible terrain artillery game. Building one teaches the same techniques Team17 used.

### The Terrain as State

The terrain bitmap IS the game state. There's no separate collision map — the pixels are the truth. `ReadPixel(x, y)` tells you if a position is sky, ground, or player. This is elegant and teaches an important concept: sometimes the display IS the data.
