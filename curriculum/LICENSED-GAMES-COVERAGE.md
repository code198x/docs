# Licensed Games: TV, Film & Media Tie-ins

**Purpose:** Document the phenomenon of licensed games - how they shaped the industry, their unique constraints, and notable examples both good and bad.

**Last Updated:** 2026-01-18

---

## Why Licensed Games Matter

Licensed games were:
1. **Industry backbone** - Funded countless studios
2. **Sales guaranteed** - Brand recognition sold units
3. **Quality variable** - From masterpieces to disasters
4. **Design challenge** - Fitting media to interactive format
5. **Publisher relationships** - Defined industry structure

Understanding licensed games means understanding how the business worked.

---

## Part 1: The Business Model

### Why Licensed Games Existed

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| **Marketing** | Film/TV marketing sold the game |
| **Recognition** | Parents bought known brands |
| **Shelf space** | Retailers favoured known properties |
| **Deadlines** | Movie release = hard deadline |
| **Risk reduction** | Publishers avoided unknown IPs |

### The Typical Deal

| Party | Role |
|-------|------|
| **License holder** | Film studio, TV network, toy company |
| **Publisher** | Paid license fee, handled distribution |
| **Developer** | Made the game (often underpaid) |
| **Retailer** | Stocked based on license, not quality |

### The Deadline Problem

Movie tie-ins had immovable deadlines:
- Game must ship with film release
- Development often started late (script changes)
- Crunch was endemic
- Quality suffered

## Part 6: LJN & The "Rainbow of Death"

### The LJN Phenomenon

LJN Toys was notorious for bad licensed games:

| Aspect | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| **Owner** | Acclaim (publisher) |
| **Strategy** | License everything, outsource development |
| **Quality** | Consistently poor |
| **Legacy** | Rainbow logo = warning sign |

**Notable LJN games:**

| Game | License | Platform | Reputation |
|------|---------|----------|------------|
| **Friday the 13th** | Friday 13th | NES | Confusing, frustrating |
| **A Nightmare on Elm Street** | Elm Street | NES | Bad |
| **Jaws** | Jaws | NES | Boring |
| **Back to the Future** | BTTF | NES | Terrible |
| **The Karate Kid** | Karate Kid | NES | Poor |
| **Who Framed Roger Rabbit** | Roger Rabbit | NES | Confusing |

**Why LJN failed:**
- License fee consumed budget
- Development outsourced to lowest bidder
- Impossible deadlines
- No quality control
- Relied on license recognition for sales

## Part 11: Design Challenges

### Adapting Non-Interactive Media

| Challenge | Solutions |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Passive source** | Identify interactive elements |
| **Pacing** | Films are 2 hours, games are longer |
| **Character abilities** | Define gameplay mechanics from source |
| **Faithful vs. Fun** | Sometimes divergence is necessary |

### What Made Good Licensed Games

| Factor | Example |
|--------|---------|
| **Adequate time** | GoldenEye had 2+ years |
| **Creative freedom** | Batman (Sunsoft) ignored film |
| **Talented developer** | Capcom, Konami, Rare |
| **License holder involvement** | Disney animation teams |
| **Genre fit** | TMNT as beat 'em up makes sense |

### What Made Bad Licensed Games

| Factor | Example |
|--------|---------|
| **Rushed deadline** | E.T. (5 weeks) |
| **Outsourced to lowest bidder** | LJN games |
| **License restrictions** | Superman 64 limitations |
| **No creative vision** | Generic game with skin |
| **Wrong genre choice** | Many mismatches |
